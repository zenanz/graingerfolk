Percy Grainger (1882-1961) was a pioneer of ethnomusicology, undertaking highly significant and unique work in an emerging field of research into folk song from around the world. He collected and transcribed English and Danish folk song, and transcribed some of the earliest wax cylinder and disc recordings of Australian Aboriginal, Polynesian and Indonesian music. Grainger was also a pioneer in new technology, using a phonograph in the field to record folk songs directly onto wax cylinders.


Grainger contributed to the development of ethnomusicology through his detailing of the singers’ personalities, society and heritage along with the music and lyrics. He arranged numerous folk melodies for a wide range of musical resources, including the well-known Country gardens, based on a Morris dance tune. Grainger studied the musical structure and dialect of the songs he recorded and faithfully observed the personalities, society and heritage of his singers. Grainger was part of a small community of folk song enthusiasts,both in Britain and further afield. He sought out other folk song collectors including Evald Tang Kristensen in Denmark and Alfred J.Knocks in New Zealand. He effectively bridged the gap between the strict musicological approach of most European folk music scholars, and the anthropological orientation of the contemporary American ethno-musicologists.


Grainger’s English Folk Song Collection which represents his initial practical experience in folk music During a total of fifty-two days scattered between April 1905 and August 1909, this indomitable Australian collected 435 English folk songs. The collection includes 216 wax cylinders, a set of extremely rare discs issued by the then Gramophone Company and the original pad and pencil notings and cylinder transcriptions of all but sixty-four of the songs collected on cylinder.


The most obvious quality of Grainger’s collecting is his enthusiasm. He wrote to a friend during the second of his one week ‘hunts’ in Lincolnshire in 1906:

. . . this ‘hunt’ is heavy work. The whole day and evening is busy writing songs down and after that one has to work for hours to get things in order and to write the many letters that go with the hunt. But the results are splendid. Twice as many and as good as last year. I have got eight songs which I would give almost anything for in order to save them from getting forgotten.

Three days later he wrote:

Today I have been a great deal on the cycle, about 50 miles, and have obtained a mass of songs. I have collected the most lovely things since I last wrote; all together about 120-150 songs. It has been hard work and has been on the whole pretty tedious, although what I have now got are precious above price.
